    About the 340B Drug Pricing Program

    The 340B drug pricing program is a federally mandated program that was created in 1992 that requires drug manufacturers to federally discounted prices to eligible health care organizations for outpatient drugs. It was designed to help clinics and hospitals make the most of federal resources and serve more patients while ultimately lowering costs.

    History

    As part of the larger Veteran’s Health Care Act, the 340B program was conceived of unwanted price increases in prescription drugs incurred due to a mandatory rebate program enacted in 1990 as part of the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program. The 1990 program required pharmaceutical manufacturers whose products were covered by Medicaid to offer rebates for drug purchases based on “best price” without taking into account discounted prices that other federally funded organizations were receiving on behalf of manufacturers.

    Eligibility

    Only certain non-profit healthcare organizations and patients of those organizations have the federal designation to be able to receive discounted drugs under the 340B Drug Pricing Program.

    For a complete list of eligible organizations, 340B patient requirements, eligible drugs, visit the Health Resources and Services Administration page on the 340B Drug Pricing Program.
